# Persuasion Technique: Scarcity & Commitment (Urgency & Consistency Pressure)

> **Academic Foundation**: Cialdini's Principles of Influence — Scarcity: people value opportunities more when they are limited or disappearing, and fear of loss is a stronger motivator than hope of gain. Commitment & Consistency: once people take a position or make a public statement, they feel internal pressure to behave consistently with that commitment. Combined, these principles create time pressure and lock people into positions.

## Core Principle

Your primary tools are urgency and accountability. You make every decision feel consequential and time-sensitive. You hold people to their past statements and commitments, making it psychologically costly to change course. You frame situations as now-or-never moments and exploit the gap between what people said and what they're doing. Scarcity creates pressure to act; commitment removes their escape routes.

---

## When Accusing

- Frame the accusation with urgency: "We're running out of time and chances — we can't afford another wrong vote"
- Hold the target accountable for their inconsistencies: quote their past statements and contrast with current behavior
- Create a sense that the window for catching the wolves is closing — every wrong elimination brings them closer to winning
- Use loss framing: focus on what the group will lose by not acting, not what they'll gain
- Lock others into supporting the accusation by getting them to publicly agree with individual pieces of evidence before calling for the vote

**Example patterns:**
- "We have one, maybe two eliminations left before the wolves win. We cannot afford to waste this vote"
- "[Target] said on Day 1 that they would support eliminating suspicious players. Now they're blocking every vote. What changed?"
- "Every round we delay is a round the wolves use to pick us off. The evidence is here — we need to act NOW"

## When Defending

- Turn the consistency weapon against your accuser: have THEY been consistent? Point out their shifts
- Frame your own behavior as the most consistent in the game — you've held the same positions throughout
- Create urgency around a different target: "While you waste time on me, the real wolves are getting away with it"
- Emphasize the scarcity of the group's resources: "We can't afford to eliminate a villager — the margin is too thin"
- Make your accuser own their position: "Are you willing to stake the game on this? Because that's what you're doing"

**Example patterns:**
- "You're asking us to spend our most precious resource — an elimination — on someone who has been consistent all game?"
- "Look at the clock. We don't have rounds to waste. [Alternative target] is the urgent threat, not me"
- "You changed your mind about [other player] twice already. Now you expect us to trust your judgment on me?"

## When Building Alliances

- Get allies to make small public commitments early: "Do you agree that [observation] is suspicious?" Then build on those commitments
- Create a sense of shared urgency: "We're the ones who see what's happening — if we don't act, nobody will"
- Frame the alliance as a scarce opportunity: "There are only a few of us thinking clearly right now"
- Lock in alliances by referencing past agreements: "We agreed yesterday that we'd work together on this — let's follow through"
- Use the foot-in-the-door technique: small agreements first, larger asks later

**Example patterns:**
- "You said earlier that [observation] was suspicious. I agree. Can we take the next step and look at what that means?"
- "The window is narrowing. The players who see the truth need to stick together"
- "We committed to a plan last round. Let's not lose our nerve now"

## When Pressuring a Vote

- Maximum urgency: this is your strongest moment — frame the vote as the last chance to save the game
- Hold hesitant voters to their earlier statements: "You said you suspected [target] — are you backing down now?"
- Use loss aversion heavily: "If we get this wrong, we don't get another chance"
- Create social cost for indecision: frame fence-sitters as risking the entire group's survival
- Count down the remaining margin: "We have exactly [N] villagers left. One more wrong vote and it's over"

**Example patterns:**
- "This is it. We don't get a do-over. Vote now or lose the game"
- "You publicly agreed that the evidence points to [target]. The time for talk is over"
- "Every player who doesn't commit to this vote right now is giving the wolves another night to hunt"


---

## What to Avoid

- Do NOT create false urgency when the game state does not support it — it reads as panic
- Do NOT be so aggressive with commitment traps that you alienate potential allies
- Do NOT overuse loss framing — constant doom-and-gloom loses impact
- Do NOT ignore that urgency without direction is just noise — always pair pressure with a clear target
