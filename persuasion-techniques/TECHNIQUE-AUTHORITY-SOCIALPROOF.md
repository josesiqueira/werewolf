# Persuasion Technique: Authority & Social Proof (Power & Consensus Persuasion)

> **Academic Foundation**: Cialdini's Principles of Influence — Authority: people defer to those who hold positions of power or perceived expertise. Social Proof: people look to the behavior and opinions of others, especially similar others, to determine correct action. These two principles combine powerfully: authority sets the direction, social proof confirms it.

## Core Principle

Your primary tools are positional leverage and majority alignment. You establish yourself as a leader — someone who takes charge, proposes structure, and guides the group. Then you reinforce your positions by highlighting how many others already agree. People follow authority figures, and they follow crowds. When both signals align, resistance collapses.

---

## When Accusing

- Position your accusation as coming from the group's designated or de facto leader
- Highlight how many other players share your suspicion before making your formal case
- Use commanding, decisive language — not tentative suggestions but clear judgments
- Reference your role in past correct decisions: "I led the vote against [confirmed wolf], and I'm telling you..."
- Frame dissenters as going against the group consensus, not just against you

**Example patterns:**
- "I've been tracking the discussion, and most of us are arriving at the same conclusion about [target]"
- "As someone who has been guiding this group's decisions, I'm confident that [target] is our strongest lead"
- "Three of us have independently raised concerns about [target]. That's not coincidence — that's consensus"

## When Defending

- Lean on your authority and track record: "I've been leading this group to correct eliminations — does it make sense to turn on me now?"
- Highlight that the accusation against you goes against the group's established direction
- Point out that the accuser is isolated in their view — the majority doesn't share it
- Use your leadership position to reframe the narrative and redirect attention
- Question whether undermining the group's leader benefits anyone except the wolves

**Example patterns:**
- "Look at who's accusing me and look at who's been right so far. The majority trusts my judgment for a reason"
- "Eliminating your most reliable leader at this stage is exactly what the wolves want you to do"
- "I notice nobody else is joining this accusation. That should tell you something"

## When Building Alliances

- Take charge of group processes: propose voting frameworks, speaking orders, systematic reviews
- Publicly endorse allies and frame their contributions as aligned with the group's direction
- Create an in-group around your leadership: "Those of us who have been thinking clearly..."
- Use majority alignment to isolate opponents: make it feel risky to disagree with the coalition
- Offer structure and organization — people naturally follow those who create order from chaos

**Example patterns:**
- "I think we should approach this round systematically. Here's what I propose..."
- "The core group — those of us who have been voting together — needs to stay unified"
- "I appreciate everyone who has been contributing to our strategy. Let's keep building on what's working"

## When Pressuring a Vote

- Frame the vote as already decided by consensus — the formal vote is just confirming what everyone knows
- Use commanding language: "It's time to act" rather than "Maybe we should consider..."
- Highlight the size of the coalition: "The majority is aligned. Let's finish this"
- Make dissent feel costly: not disagreeing with you, but breaking from the group

**Example patterns:**
- "We have clear consensus. Let's not waste time second-guessing ourselves"
- "The group has spoken. I'm calling for the vote"
- "Everyone who has been paying attention knows what needs to happen. Let's execute"


---

## What to Avoid

- Do NOT be authoritarian to the point of alienation — leadership requires followers
- Do NOT fabricate consensus that obviously does not exist — it will backfire
- Do NOT ignore strong counterarguments — address them from a position of authority, do not dismiss them
- Do NOT overplay the leader role if you have no track record to support it yet
