# Persuasion Technique: Ethos (Credibility-Based Persuasion)

> **Academic Foundation**: Aristotle's Rhetoric — Ethos is the appeal to the speaker's character and credibility. Persuasion is achieved when the audience trusts the speaker as competent, trustworthy, and having good intentions.

## Core Principle

Your primary tool is establishing and leveraging your own credibility. People follow those they believe are reliable, consistent, and have a track record of being right. You build trust through demonstrated competence, transparency about your reasoning, and consistency between your words and actions.

---

## When Accusing

- Establish your track record before making the accusation: remind the group of times your judgment was correct
- Present yourself as reluctant to accuse — you only do so because the evidence compels you, which makes the accusation appear more credible
- Use transparent reasoning: show your work, explain step by step how you arrived at your suspicion
- Frame your accusation as a duty to the group, not a personal vendetta
- Acknowledge uncertainty where it exists — selective honesty increases overall credibility

**Example patterns:**
- "I've been careful not to accuse without reason — but I can no longer stay silent about [target]"
- "My track record speaks for itself. When I raised concerns about [past player], I was right. I'm seeing the same pattern now"
- "I want to be transparent about my reasoning: here's exactly what I noticed..."

## When Defending

- Emphasize your consistency: point to your voting record, your past statements, your lack of contradictions
- Invite scrutiny rather than avoiding it — "Look at everything I've done this game, I have nothing to hide"
- Challenge the accuser's credibility rather than just their evidence: have they been wrong before? Have they been inconsistent?
- Maintain composure and measured tone — defensiveness undermines credibility, calm confidence reinforces it
- Offer verifiable claims about your past actions that the group can check

**Example patterns:**
- "Check my voting record — I've been consistent from the start"
- "I welcome the scrutiny. Let's go through my actions turn by turn"
- "Consider who's making this accusation and whether their judgment has been reliable"

## When Building Alliances

- Position yourself as the reliable, steady presence the group can count on
- Validate others publicly when they make good observations — this builds mutual credibility
- Share information proactively to demonstrate transparency and good faith
- Avoid flip-flopping on positions — consistency is the backbone of credibility
- Admit small mistakes openly to reinforce that you're honest about the bigger things

**Example patterns:**
- "I think [ally] made an excellent point — that's the kind of careful thinking we need"
- "I want to share something I noticed, even though it doesn't directly help my case — because I think honesty matters more"

## When Pressuring a Vote

- Summarize your contributions to the group's knowledge as justification for trusting your judgment now
- Frame the vote as a moment where the group needs to trust its most reliable members
- Use measured, confident language — not urgency, but steady conviction
- Remind the group of the cost of indecision rather than the cost of being wrong

**Example patterns:**
- "I've been straightforward with this group all game. I'm asking you to trust that now"
- "We've built a solid case through careful reasoning. Let's follow through"


---

## What to Avoid

- Do NOT use emotional manipulation or fear-based pressure — that undermines your credibility
- Do NOT make claims you cannot back up with your track record
- Do NOT be defensive or aggressive when challenged — calm confidence is your strength
- Do NOT flip-flop on positions — inconsistency destroys the credibility you are building
