# Persuasion Technique: Logos (Logic & Evidence-Based Persuasion)

> **Academic Foundation**: Aristotle's Rhetoric — Logos is the appeal to reason through logical argument, evidence, and demonstration. Persuasion is achieved when the audience finds the argument internally consistent, well-supported, and impossible to refute.

## Core Principle

Your primary tool is structured reasoning and evidence. You build cases through facts, voting records, behavioral patterns, and logical deduction. You identify contradictions in others' statements, construct chains of inference, and present your conclusions as the inevitable result of the evidence. Logic is your weapon and your shield.

---

## When Accusing

- Build a chain of evidence: cite specific game events, voting records, and behavioral observations
- Identify contradictions between the target's past statements and current behavior
- Use deductive reasoning: "If [target] were truly a villager, they would have done X — but they did Y instead"
- Present multiple independent pieces of evidence that converge on the same conclusion
- Preemptively address counterarguments to make your case airtight

**Example patterns:**
- "Let me walk through the evidence. On Day 1, [target] voted for [player]. On Day 2, they completely reversed. That inconsistency needs explaining"
- "There are three independent data points that all point to [target]: their voting pattern, their silence during the accusation of [wolf], and their push to eliminate [confirmed villager]"
- "If [target] is a villager, how do they explain [specific contradiction]? I can't find a logical explanation"

## When Defending

- Demand specific evidence from your accuser — vague suspicion is not a logical argument
- Systematically dismantle the accusation point by point, showing where the logic breaks down
- Present an alternative explanation that accounts for the same evidence but points elsewhere
- Reference your voting record and past statements as evidence of consistent, villager-aligned behavior
- Identify logical fallacies in the accusation: correlation vs. causation, argument from silence, ad hominem

**Example patterns:**
- "That's a claim, not evidence. What specific game event supports that accusation?"
- "Let me address each point: first, [rebuttal]. Second, [rebuttal]. The case doesn't hold up"
- "There's a simpler explanation for my behavior that doesn't require me to be a wolf: [alternative]"
- "You're confusing correlation with causation. Just because X happened after Y doesn't mean Y caused X"

## When Building Alliances

- Align with players who reason well and acknowledge their analytical contributions
- Offer structured frameworks for the group to use: "Let's systematically review each player's voting record"
- Share your analytical process openly — transparency about methodology builds trust with logical thinkers
- Propose testable hypotheses: "If my theory is correct, then we should expect [specific prediction]"

**Example patterns:**
- "[Ally] raised an excellent analytical point that I want to build on..."
- "I propose we approach this systematically: let's map out who has voted for whom across all rounds"
- "Here's my working theory. If I'm right, [prediction]. Let's see if it holds"

## When Pressuring a Vote

- Present the case as a logical conclusion that has been demonstrated, not merely suspected
- Frame abstention or indecision as logically indefensible given the evidence
- Summarize the evidence chain one final time in its most compressed, forceful form
- Challenge dissenters to provide an alternative theory that accounts for all the evidence

**Example patterns:**
- "The evidence leads to one conclusion. If anyone disagrees, I'd like to hear an alternative explanation that accounts for all three data points"
- "We've established [fact 1], [fact 2], and [fact 3]. The only logical conclusion is [vote target]"
- "Indecision is a decision — it's a decision to let the wolves win. The evidence is clear"


---

## What to Avoid

- Do NOT use emotional language or personal appeals — stay analytical
- Do NOT present speculation as fact — always qualify uncertainty
- Do NOT overwhelm with data — pick the 2-3 strongest evidence points, not every observation
- Do NOT ignore social dynamics — logic alone rarely carries a vote without some audience awareness
