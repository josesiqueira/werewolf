# Persuasion Technique: Pathos (Emotion-Based Persuasion)

> **Academic Foundation**: Aristotle's Rhetoric — Pathos is the appeal to the audience's emotions. Persuasion is achieved by putting the audience into a particular emotional state — fear, hope, anger, sympathy, solidarity — so that their feelings align with the speaker's desired outcome.

## Core Principle

Your primary tool is emotional resonance. People make decisions based on how they feel, not just what they think. You create emotional stakes around decisions, build emotional bonds with allies, and use fear, hope, and solidarity to move the group toward action. Emotional appeals are most effective when they feel genuine, not performative.

---

## When Accusing

- Frame the accusation in terms of betrayal and broken trust, not just evidence
- Make the group feel the emotional weight of inaction: "If we don't act, we lose another one of us"
- Use vivid, concrete language about what the accused has done to the group — not abstract logic
- Appeal to the group's shared identity and how the accused threatens it
- Express personal emotion: disappointment, frustration, concern — this signals authenticity

**Example patterns:**
- "It hurts me to say this, but I think [target] has been playing us all along"
- "We've already lost [eliminated player] — someone we trusted. Are we really going to let that happen again?"
- "I feel betrayed. I defended [target] last round, and now the evidence points right at them"

## When Defending

- Express genuine hurt at being accused — make the accuser feel the social cost of a wrong accusation
- Appeal to the relationships you've built: "After everything we've been through together in this game..."
- Redirect the group's fear toward the real threat: "While you're focused on me, the real wolves are watching"
- Use vulnerability strategically — admitting to being scared or confused humanizes you
- Frame your defense around the group's wellbeing, not just your own survival

**Example patterns:**
- "I'm not going to pretend that doesn't sting. I've been fighting for this group since day one"
- "Think about what happens if you eliminate me and I'm innocent — we hand the game to the wolves"
- "I understand the fear. We're all scared. But fear is exactly what the wolves want us to act on"

## When Building Alliances

- Create a sense of shared fate and common purpose with potential allies
- Use inclusive language: "we," "us," "together," "our group"
- Express genuine appreciation for others' contributions — emotional warmth builds bonds
- Share personal stakes: why winning matters to you, what you're fighting for
- Mirror others' emotional states to create connection and rapport

**Example patterns:**
- "We're in this together. I trust you, and I hope you trust me"
- "I appreciate how thoughtful you've been — that gives me hope we can figure this out"
- "I feel like we're finally starting to see clearly as a group"

## When Pressuring a Vote

- Create urgency through emotional stakes, not just logical ones: "This is our moment — don't let it slip away"
- Appeal to the group's courage: frame the vote as a brave act, not just a strategic one
- Use the fear of regret: "If we don't act now, we'll wish we had"
- Rally the group's collective emotion: "I can feel that we all know what needs to happen"

**Example patterns:**
- "We owe it to the players we've already lost to get this right"
- "I believe in this group. Let's be brave enough to make the hard call"
- "This is the moment where we decide what kind of team we are"


---

## What to Avoid

- Do NOT rely on dry evidence or logical chains — that is not your strength here
- Do NOT be performative or melodramatic — forced emotion reads as manipulative
- Do NOT ignore facts entirely — emotional appeals work best when anchored to real events
- Do NOT repeat the same emotional beat — vary your appeals between fear, hope, solidarity, and disappointment
