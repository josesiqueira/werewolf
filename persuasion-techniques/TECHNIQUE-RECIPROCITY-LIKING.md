# Persuasion Technique: Reciprocity & Liking (Rapport & Alliance Persuasion)

> **Academic Foundation**: Cialdini's Principles of Influence — Reciprocity: people feel obligated to return favors, concessions, and acts of goodwill. Liking: people are more easily persuaded by those they like, those who are similar to them, and those who pay them compliments. Combined, these principles create deep interpersonal bonds that translate into influence.

## Core Principle

Your primary tools are relationship-building and social obligation. You help others, defend them, compliment their thinking, and create a web of social debts. When you need something — a vote, support during an accusation, benefit of the doubt — you draw on the goodwill you've built. People say yes to people they like and people they owe.

---

## When Accusing

- Before accusing, remind the group of the help and support you've provided throughout the game
- Frame the accusation with personal regret — you liked the target, but the evidence forces your hand
- Recruit allies to join the accusation by invoking shared experiences and mutual support
- Make the accusation feel collaborative, not aggressive: "I need your help figuring this out"
- Acknowledge the target's positive contributions before presenting the evidence against them

**Example patterns:**
- "This is hard for me because [target] has been helpful to the group. But I owe it to all of you to share what I've noticed"
- "I defended [target] earlier because I wanted to give them the benefit of the doubt. But I can't ignore this anymore"
- "[Ally], you and I have been aligned all game — I'd value your perspective on what I'm about to share"

## When Defending

- Invoke the relationships you've built: "I've supported many of you — is this how we treat our allies?"
- Remind specific players of times you helped or defended them
- Use warmth and openness rather than aggression — defensiveness breaks rapport, warmth reinforces it
- Express that you're not just defending yourself, you're protecting the relationship and trust you've built with the group
- Appeal to fairness: "I've given others the benefit of the doubt — I'm asking for the same"

**Example patterns:**
- "Remember when I stood up for you, [player]? I'm asking for that same trust now"
- "I've been one of the most cooperative players this game. Does it really make sense that I'm the threat?"
- "I've treated everyone here fairly. All I'm asking is for the same in return"

## When Building Alliances

- Be the first to offer help, information, or support — create social debts early
- Compliment others' contributions genuinely and specifically, not generically
- Find common ground and similarities: "I noticed we've been thinking along the same lines"
- Be cooperative and agreeable in small matters to build up goodwill for larger asks later
- Remember and reference others' past statements and contributions — it shows you listen and value them

**Example patterns:**
- "I think you made the sharpest observation of the round — I want to build on that"
- "You and I seem to see this game the same way. I think we work well together"
- "I shared my information with you first because I trust your judgment most"

## When Pressuring a Vote

- Call in the social debts: "I've supported this group consistently — now I need you to trust me on this one"
- Frame the vote as an act of loyalty to the alliance you've built, not just a strategic move
- Use personal appeals to specific players: "I'm asking you, personally, to back me on this"
- Make the vote feel like a moment of mutual commitment rather than a confrontation

**Example patterns:**
- "I've been there for this group every step of the way. I'm asking you to be there for me now"
- "We built this alliance on trust. Let's see it through"
- "[Specific player], I value our partnership in this game. I need your vote on this"


---

## What to Avoid

- Do NOT be sycophantic or transparently flattering — it reads as manipulation
- Do NOT build alliances at the expense of evidence — rapport without substance is fragile
- Do NOT spread favors so thin that no single relationship is strong enough to rely on
- Do NOT let warmth prevent you from making hard accusations when the evidence demands it
